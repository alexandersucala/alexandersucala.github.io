Systems

I build systems that keep research and AI programs reliable while you improve fast — so you don’t accidentally trust a “success” or "correct answer" that was actually wrong.

What these systems are for

In real research and real software, the worst failures are the quiet ones — the ones you think are correct. A program finishes, you get results or feedback, and only later you realize something was wrong: the inputs changed, a setting was different, or it just failed silently.

These systems are designed to make that almost impossible. They catch problems early, keep a clear record of what happened, and make results repeatable no matter what AI you use, when you use it, or where you run it from.

In short, I design AI systems that either work correctly or fail loudly — so the mistake is obvious and can be fixed instead of quietly happening again.

Core systems

AUFG research system

AUFG runs like a lab program. Each experiment creates a it's own historical data, making it easy to reproduce and compare results across changes. The goal isn’t impressive charts — it’s trustworthy measurement.

Fail-closed execution

If something is wrong, the run is marked invalid and stops early. This prevents results that look finished but are actually based on bad assumptions.

Artifact-first records

Every attempt leaves behind a clear record. Even failed runs produce useful evidence, making debugging faster and decisions easier to defend later.

Deterministic randomness and enforced interfaces

Randomness is controlled instead of left to chance. Inputs and interfaces are enforced so small, silent changes don’t sneak in unnoticed.

Novelty checking against prior work

New ideas are checked against a trusted library of prior papers and patents. This helps catch overlap early and speeds up iteration: check → decide → adjust.

Exploratory research infrastructure

Long-term exploration needs more than basic testing. These systems protect how tests are ran and clarity while still letting work move quickly.
